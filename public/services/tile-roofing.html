Concrete vs Clay / Composite Tile

Tile roofing is one of the most popular choices in South Florida, prized for its durability, energy efficiency, and timeless Mediterranean appearance. However, not all tile roofs are created equal. Understanding the differences between concrete, clay, and composite tile systems is essential for making an informed decision.

Concrete Tile Roofing

Concrete tile is the most common tile roofing material in South Florida. Made from sand, cement, and pigments, concrete tiles are durable, cost-effective, and available in a wide range of colors and profiles.

Advantages: Lower initial cost compared to clay, excellent wind resistance when properly installed, good thermal performance, and long lifespan (30–50 years with proper maintenance).

Considerations: Concrete tiles are porous and can absorb moisture, leading to algae growth, color fading, and eventual surface degradation if not maintained. They are also heavier than other roofing materials, requiring proper structural support.

Clay Tile Roofing (Premium Option)

Clay tile is the premium choice for South Florida homeowners seeking the highest level of durability, color retention, and authentic Mediterranean styling. Made from natural clay fired at high temperatures, these tiles are denser, harder, and more resistant to moisture absorption than concrete.

Advantages: Superior longevity (50–100 years), excellent color retention, minimal maintenance, non-porous surface resists algae and mold, and timeless aesthetic appeal that enhances property value.

Considerations: Higher initial cost compared to concrete tile. However, when lifecycle costs, maintenance, and resale value are considered, clay tile often proves to be the most cost-effective choice for long-term property ownership.

Composite & Synthetic Tile (Premium Alternative)

High-end composite tile systems use advanced polymer technology to replicate the appearance of clay or slate while offering lighter weight and improved impact resistance. These systems are ideal for properties requiring the look of clay tile without the structural load.

Advantages: Significantly lighter than concrete or clay, excellent wind and impact resistance, Class A fire rating, minimal maintenance, and often come with enhanced warranties.

Underlayment & Flashing Matter More Than the Tile

Here's what most homeowners and property managers don't realize: in South Florida, tile roofs almost never fail because the tiles themselves break or wear out. They fail because water gets underneath the tiles due to poor underlayment, improper flashing details, or inadequate valley and penetration waterproofing.

Tile is simply the decorative and protective top layer. The real waterproofing system lies beneath — and that's where All Phase Construction USA focuses our attention and expertise.

Our Tile Roof Waterproofing System Includes:

  • • High-Performance Synthetic Underlayment: We install premium synthetic underlayment engineered for high-temperature environments and long-term UV exposure. These materials far outperform traditional felt paper.
  • • Secondary Water Barriers in Critical Areas: Self-adhering membranes are installed in valleys, along eaves, around penetrations, and at wall transitions to provide backup waterproofing where water concentrates.
  • • Code-Compliant Flashing Systems: Proper metal flashing at walls, chimneys, skylights, and roof-to-wall transitions prevents the most common leak points in tile roofs.
  • • Valley Metal & Pan Systems: We install engineered valley pans or metal valley systems designed to handle high water flow during South Florida's intense rainstorms.
  • • Proper Ventilation: Adequate attic ventilation prevents heat and moisture buildup that can degrade underlayment and decking from below.

All Phase Construction USA does not cut corners on underlayment or flashing. These hidden systems are the difference between a tile roof that lasts 20 years and one that lasts 50+ years. Our detailed proposals clearly specify the underlayment and waterproofing materials we use, so you know exactly what you're getting.

Tile Roof Replacement Process

All Phase Construction USA follows a systematic tile roof replacement process designed to ensure proper waterproofing, code compliance, and long-term performance:

1. Tile Removal & Deck Inspection

We carefully remove existing tiles and underlayment, then thoroughly inspect the roof deck for rot, water damage, or structural issues. Any damaged sheathing is repaired or replaced before proceeding.

2. Deck Fastening & Structural Upgrades

If required by HVHZ code or inspection findings, we re-nail or re-screw the deck to ensure proper attachment to roof framing. This step is critical for wind resistance and often required for insurance and permitting.

3. High-Performance Underlayment Installation

We install premium synthetic underlayment across the entire roof deck, with secondary water barriers in valleys, eaves, and critical areas. This creates a complete waterproof membrane beneath the tile.

4. Flashing, Valley, & Penetration Details

All flashing at walls, chimneys, vents, and skylights is installed using code-compliant methods and materials. Valley metal or pan systems are installed and sealed before tile placement.

5. Batten System Installation (If Applicable)

Some tile systems use horizontal battens to create air space beneath the tiles for ventilation and drainage. We install treated wood or composite battens according to manufacturer specifications when required.

6. Tile Installation & Fastening

Tiles are installed from eave to ridge using HVHZ-compliant fastening methods. Every tile is mechanically attached with corrosion-resistant fasteners to meet Florida's strict wind-load requirements.

7. Ridge, Hip, & Accessory Installation

Ridge caps, hip tiles, and other accessories are installed with mortar or foam closure systems and mechanically fastened. Proper ridge ventilation is integrated to ensure adequate attic airflow.

8. Final Inspections & Documentation

We coordinate all building department inspections and provide a complete close-out package including permits, inspection reports, product warranties, and wind-mitigation documentation for insurance purposes.

HVHZ Compliance & Wind Resistance

All tile roof installations in Broward and Palm Beach Counties must meet Florida Building Code HVHZ requirements. This includes:

  • • Mechanical Fastening: Every tile must be mechanically attached using corrosion-resistant fasteners rated for coastal environments.
  • • Engineered Fastening Schedules: The number, type, and placement of fasteners must meet or exceed wind-uplift calculations for your property's location and roof geometry.
  • • Product Approval: All tiles, underlayment, and accessories must carry Florida Product Approval numbers demonstrating they have passed required testing.
  • • Secondary Water Resistance: Code-compliant underlayment systems that provide backup waterproofing even if tiles are displaced during a storm.

As a dual-licensed contractor, All Phase Construction USA handles all engineering, permitting, and inspection coordination required for HVHZ-compliant tile roof installations.
